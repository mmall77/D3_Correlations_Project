Correlations Discovered Between Health Risks and Age, Income

The data shows that there is a negative correlation between income and smoking. The higher the income the lower incidence of smoking. Smoking skews to an older age group from the data. As income decreases smoking tendencies tend to increase. It seems also the preponderance of the smoking habit is in the southern US. Obesity also shows a negative correlation. Obesity goes down as income increases. Not surprisingly, the northeastern states in general have the highest income per capita and less obesity and in the southern states with their depressed economies there are more overweight people. It would be interesting to add in education level to see how that affects obesity and smoking. We would need to add in an extra dataset to see how that affects this chart.

There is a correlation between median incomes per capita and level of health coverage. MA seems to have one of the highest per capita median incomes with the lowest incidence of people without healthcare. MS has the lowest per capita income of all the states and a high incidence of people without healthcare. TX has a middling income per capita but the highest incidence of non health coverage. The positive relation between poverty and healthcare is the most striking. All the southern states are well represented with high levels of poverty and lack of healthcare.
